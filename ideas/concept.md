I actually think this is a stronger concept than trying to make *Polana* do everything.

To me, **Polana** feels like lunch, dinner, families, brunch, cultured dairy, fresh herbs, pierogi, soups, vegetables, and lighter contemporary Polish cuisine.

A separate pub lets you lean into a completely different side of Eastern Europe—the warm, dark, social side.

## The feeling

Not a sports bar.

Not a German beer hall.

Not an Irish pub.

Think of it as **a neighborhood tavern somewhere between Kraków, Prague, Vilnius, and Lviv**, interpreted through modern American craft cocktail culture.

The atmosphere should feel like:

* reclaimed oak
* dark green tile
* blackened steel
* brass
* amber lighting
* candles
* old maps
* linen curtains
* heavy wooden tables
* leather banquettes
* shelves of bottles and preserved fruit
* vintage travel posters from Warsaw, Riga, Tallinn, Kraków, and Prague

Instead of TVs everywhere, maybe one small television hidden away. Conversation is the entertainment.

# Relationship to Polana

This is the part I find especially compelling.

They're not competitors—they're complements.

**Polana**

* Daylight
* Forest glade
* Fresh herbs
* Yogurt
* Twaróg
* Soups
* Salads
* Families
* Brunch
* Contemporary Polish cuisine

**Amber & Rye**

* Candlelight
* Cellar
* Oak
* Smoke
* Vodka
* Mead
* Beer
* Cocktails
* Shared plates
* Evenings

They feel like two expressions of the same cultural identity: one inspired by the openness of a forest clearing, the other by the warmth of gathering around a table after sunset.

One additional thought: if *Polana* succeeds, *Amber & Rye* could become more than a pub—it could be the social "living room" of the brand. Guests might have dinner at Polana and then walk next door for a nalewka flight, a glass of Baltic porter, and a slice of sernik. That kind of pairing could create a destination rather than just two separate businesses.
